Deep Dive: What You Get (and What You Don’t)

Amazon SES

Infrastructure

SES is the “mail engine” — built for reliability, scale, and cost efficiency. Ideal when your server (Plesk) should act like a mail client and relay through a trusted network.

Highlights

  • Pay-as-you-go pricing, extremely cost-efficient
  • Excellent fit for transactional email + multi-site servers
  • Supports DKIM, feedback loops (bounces/complaints), API + SMTP

Trade-offs

  • More “AWS-ish” setup and ops
  • Dashboards exist, but not as friendly as platform tools
  • Template/marketing tooling is not the focus
Practical review: Best for hosting environments where you want one SMTP relay that fixes Outlook deliverability across many sites without paying platform pricing.

SendGrid (Twilio)

Platform

SendGrid is a full email platform with dashboards, templates, and easier administration. It’s often the smoothest experience for teams that want UI + analytics.

Highlights

  • Very easy onboarding and management UI
  • Good analytics and event tooling
  • Strong for both transactional and (optionally) marketing

Trade-offs

  • More expensive at scale than SES
  • You pay for platform features you may not use
  • Tiered plans can be less flexible
Practical review: Best if you want “set it up once + monitor in dashboards” and don’t mind paying extra for convenience.

Mailgun

Developer-First

Mailgun is API-centric and strong for dev workflows, analytics, and email tooling. Great for SaaS/app teams that want more control and insight.

Highlights

  • Strong APIs + webhooks
  • Good deliverability tooling and analytics
  • Useful add-ons for verification and reputation monitoring

Trade-offs

  • Can get pricey depending on plan/volume
  • Often more than a typical hosting server needs
  • Some tooling is packaged into separate products/tiers
Practical review: Great if you’re building an application and need deep mail operations. For “multi-site Plesk SMTP relay,” it can be overkill.

Proton Mail

Mailbox + Privacy

Proton is primarily a secure mailbox provider. It’s excellent for human communication and privacy, but it’s not designed to be your primary transactional email relay for websites.

Highlights

  • Privacy-first mailbox provider
  • Great for business email accounts and secure communications

Trade-offs

  • Transactional/SMTP relay use is not its core product focus
  • Sending limits and anti-abuse controls are mailbox-oriented
  • Limited tooling for bounce/complaint workflows at scale
Practical review: Proton is a strong mailbox solution. For your hosting SMTP relay needs, it’s not the right tool compared to SES/SendGrid/Mailgun.

Deliverability reality (Outlook/Hotmail)

For shared hosting servers, the biggest win is: stop sending directly from your server IP. Use a relay with strong reputation and proper authentication (SPF/DKIM/DMARC).

  • SES: great for relay + cost (requires more setup)
  • SendGrid: great dashboards + faster admin workflow
  • Mailgun: strong APIs + dev tooling
  • Proton: not built for server transactional flows

Deliverability & Reputation

Getting email accepted by Outlook/Gmail is only step one. Getting email into the Inbox requires authentication, reputation, and engagement.

The Email Trust Stack

Every modern email provider scores your domain using these signals:

  • SPF — Authorises servers allowed to send mail
  • DKIM — Cryptographic signing proving domain identity
  • DMARC — Policy tying SPF + DKIM together
  • IP reputation — History of the sending network
  • Domain reputation — History of your domain
  • User engagement — Opens, replies, “Not spam” clicks
Modern inbox placement is mostly about reputation, not content.

Why servers get blocked (Outlook example)

  • New server IP with no history
  • Missing DKIM or DMARC
  • Sending directly from VPS/shared hosting IP
  • Low engagement or test-style emails
SMTP relay providers fix this by sending from large trusted IP pools with established reputation.

Plesk Smart-Host Setup Notes (High Level)

Your goal is simple: make Postfix/Plesk relay outbound mail through the provider, instead of sending directly from your server IP.

Minimum DNS checklist

  • SPF: authorise your provider (e.g., include their SPF)
  • DKIM: enable signing and publish the DKIM DNS records
  • DMARC: publish policy record (start with p=none)

This is the foundation for Outlook/Gmail trust. If any of these are missing, junk placement becomes more likely.

Operational checklist

  • Use an SMTP relay provider (SES/SendGrid/Mailgun) as your “smart host”
  • Warm up domains if new (steady consistent sending)
  • Monitor bounces/complaints and keep rates low
  • Avoid “test-looking” subjects for warmup (use real transactional patterns)

Once Microsoft accepts mail (no more 550 blocks), inbox placement becomes reputation + engagement.
